\noindent
\textbf{Problem 1.}
Real numbers $a_1, a_2, \dots, a_n$ are given.  
For each $i$ $(1 \le i \le n)$ define
\[
d_i = \max\{a_j : 1 \le j \le i\} - \min\{a_j : i \le j \le n\}
\]
and let
\[
d = \max\{d_i : 1 \le i \le n\}.
\]

\begin{enumerate}[label=(\alph*)]
\item Prove that for any real numbers
$x_1 \le x_2 \le \cdots \le x_n$,
\[
\max\{|x_i - a_i| : 1 \le i \le n\} \ge \frac{d}{2}.
\]

\item Show that there exist real numbers
$x_1 \le x_2 \le \cdots \le x_n$
such that equality holds.
\end{enumerate}

\vspace{0.7cm}

\noindent
\textbf{Problem 2.}
Consider five points $A, B, C, D, E$ such that $ABCD$ is a parallelogram
and $BCED$ is a cyclic quadrilateral.
Let $\ell$ be a line passing through $A$.
Suppose that $\ell$ intersects the interior of segment $DC$ at $F$
and intersects line $BC$ at $G$.
Suppose also that $EF = EG = EC$.
Prove that $\ell$ is the bisector of angle $DAB$.

\vspace{0.7cm}

\noindent
\textbf{Problem 3.}
In a mathematical competition some competitors are friends.
Friendship is always mutual.
Call a group of competitors a \emph{clique}
if each two of them are friends.
The number of members of a clique is called its \emph{size}.
Given that the largest size of a clique is even,
prove that the competitors can be arranged in two rooms
such that the largest clique in one room
has the same size as the largest clique in the other room.

\noindent
\textbf{Problem 4.}
In triangle $ABC$ the bisector of angle $BCA$
intersects the circumcircle again at $R$,
the perpendicular bisector of $BC$ at $P$,
and the perpendicular bisector of $AC$ at $Q$.
The midpoint of $BC$ is $K$ and the midpoint of $AC$ is $L$.
Prove that triangles $RPK$ and $RQL$ have the same area.

\vspace{0.7cm}

\noindent
\textbf{Problem 5.}
Let $a$ and $b$ be positive integers.
Show that if $4ab - 1$ divides $(4a^2 - 1)^2$,
then $a = b$.

\vspace{0.7cm}

\noindent
\textbf{Problem 6.}
Let $n$ be a positive integer.
Consider
\[
S = \{(x,y,z) : x,y,z \in \{0,1,\dots,n\},\ x+y+z>0\}
\]
as a set of $(n+1)^3 - 1$ points in three-dimensional space.
Determine the smallest possible number of planes
whose union contains $S$ but does not include $(0,0,0)$.
